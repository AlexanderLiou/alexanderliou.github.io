Stereolithography Small Batch Material Testing Apparatus

Summary

This apparatus interfaces with industrial SLA 3D printers to downsize their operations to enable small batch material testing. The machine maintains high print resolution and requires minimal change in operation procedure from production mode. I developed this device as a mechanical engineering intern at Protocafe Inc, a rapid prototyping company in California. Major requirements included testing with a minimized volume of batch-testing material, easy swapping between testing and production configurations, easy cleaning and maintenance, and minimal invasive modifications of the industrial printer.

  • Facilitates 98% savings on development material during testing operations. This is critical, as small batches of material enable rapid and minor changes between them for easy optimization.
  • Predicted to save 90% cost of production materials after testing is completed.

How

  • Designed using Solidworks and built using in-house fabricated 3D printed parts, COTS hardware, and machined plates.
  • Self-directed independent project. Communicated with management to determine objectives, contraints, and resources before executing project.
  • 5 Major subsystems to compartmentalize complexity, intricate details withheld as proprietary.

Challenges

  • Timing of the project was unpredictable at times due to the necessity for prototyping mechanisms and iterating upon failures.
  • Extremely small build volume relative to production printer vat necessitated much finer resin volumetric flow control to maintain level precision. Achieved mechanically to avoid invasively modifying the industrial printer.
  • Spring-loaded precision sensor triggers designed and installed to stop one mechanism from crashing into other components.
  • Backlash in mechanical and fluid systems led to longer-than-expected printing times, but print times for tests were still significantly shorter than production operations.
  • High viscosity of resin led to complications necessitating geometric changes to compensate.

Details

The (prototype) resin pump below uses a stepper motor wired into the control loop of the printer to maintain the level of resin in the printer to extremely tight tolerance. It does so through a gear reduction and lead screw driving a syringe, pushing resin through a hose into the vat. An over-centering crash-shifting gear lever enables disengagement of the mechanism for manual adjustment.

The vat below was optimized to minimize testing resin material required per batch while maintaining acceptable clearances. It housed a liquid polymer that solidifies under a UV laser, and its level is precisely controlled by a custom resin pump. The tributary off the corner of the vat is for sensing purposes. The vat itself features a resin flow valve with a barb fitting. It is bolted to a quick-swapping scaffolding to fit into a mounting system designed for stock printing vats. The scaffold material and fabrication process facilitated strength and durability.

The elevator mount and build platorm below controls z-axis positioning of the build. It features a build area just large enough to fit a part for testing quality and properties.

The sweeper mechanism is simply a precise blade to move resin to maintain layer precision. The bumper trigger below stops the blade from traveling too far, as it would crash into custom mechanisms described above. These spring-loaded SLA 3D printed parts contain a linear mechanism to push a barrier into a beam-break sensor, spoofing the printer into believing end of travel has been reached.
