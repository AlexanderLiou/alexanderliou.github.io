VTOL Mk 1 Aircraft

Summary

This aircraft was designed to fly in two flight modes: hover and fixed wing forward flight. It utilizes a tricopter configuration with two front rotors that tilt on servo mechanisms to control yaw in hover and to produce thrust in forward flight. The aircraft was originally aerodynamically sized for a 2.5kg MTOW, but ended up weighing only 2.0kg, lowering cruise and stall speed. It is primarily constructed of lightweight 3D printed PLA, with carbon fiber tubes as the spar and boom. The controller for the aircraft is a teensy 4.0 microcontroller, using an MPU 6050 gyroscope and standard RC radio PWM receiver. The teensy outputs PWM signals to both the servo motors and brushless motor ESC’s to control the aircraft in both flight domains. A slight dihedral to the wingtips adds to roll stability, and a V-tail is employed for efficient, low drag pitch and yaw control.

Design Process

    At first, I estimated mass and used hand calculations and a Matlab script to determine a reasonable wing sizing for different aspect ratios and wing loadings. Given that stall speed did not vary significantly with AR, a more efficient AR of 6 was chosen, yielding a slightly more aggressive wing loading of 6 kg/m^2.

    Although originally planning a 2.5kg aircraft, some quick mass estimates compelled me to use 1.6kg in calculations. This yielded a chord of 0.226m, span of 1.342m, and wetted area S of 0.3m2. I took into consideration stall speed to ensure that the aircraft would not need to fly excessively fast to maintain lift. Stall was calculated at a wing angle of attack around 10 degrees as a conservative measure. I designed the wing using a Clark-Y airfoil, commonly found in RC aircraft, with a maximum lift-to-drag ratio over 70 at a Reynolds number of 200000. Using Cl at 10 degrees and Re=200000, stall speed was calculated to be 8.38m/s. Using NASA’s Vehicle Sketch Pad for quick low fidelity simulations, I iterated upon designs until I achieved an aircraft with stable properties and high L/D. The wing was primarily rectangular with slight taper to stay true to calculations. The tail was designed with a -3 deg angle of incidence to the aircraft to balance the pitching moment and trim to the correct pitch angle.

    In forward flight, the aircraft is aerodynamically stable in pitch and yaw, and trims to an overall angle of attack of 0.144 degrees. At cruise speed, this produces enough lift for the aircraft to fly in steady level flight given a 2 degree angle of incidence of the wing mounted to the fuselage. After complete mechanical build, the mass of the aircraft was measured at 2.0 kg. I also decided to change the intended aircraft cruise angle from zero to 2.75 degrees in order to operate at maximum L/D of the wing. At its final MTOW of 2.0kg, cruise speed changes to 11 m/s at an aircraft angle of attack of 2.75 degrees and the wing at 4.75 degrees. Stall speed at this AoA changes to 8.81m/s.

    Following initial outer aero design and iteration, I began the long process of detailed mechanical design, which was the bulk of the time spent on this project. Much consideration was given to ease of 3D printing with minimal support, and ensuring that the most important aerodynamic surfaces had accurate outer contours. In total, the mechanical design incorporates four control surfaces (two ailerons and two ruddervators), three brushless motors (one fixed, two tiltrotor), and six servo motors (four for control surfaces and two for tiltrotor). All design was completed using Autodesk Fusion.

Controller

    The flight controller code was based off of dRehm Flight VTOL, an open source Arduino C program with the building blocks to create a flight controller from a Teensy 4.0 microcontroller. I heavily modified the flight controller code to implment my own tricopter controller and fixed wing controller that work with my configuration of motors and servos. I added features that enable me to switch between flight modes using switches on the radio.

    In hover mode, PID control is used to maintain a desired pitch angle, roll angle, and yaw angular rate using the MPU6050 gyro data and comparing it to the radio input, representing desired states. Contrary to a conventional quadcopter, this tricopter angles the front two tiltrotors to produce yawing moments for yaw control. Pitcha and roll control are achieved through differential thrust of the propeller motors.

In fixed wing flight, I implemented two modes: direct control surface manipulation and stabilized flight. In the former, radio inputs are routed directly to the control surface servos with some scaling factors and position corrections. This requires a good pilot to fly well. In the latter mode, accessible via a switch on the radio, the control surfaces are PID-controlled to return the aircraft to steady level flight. The video below demonstrates these features.

Challenges

  • Manual PID tuning of gain values for my specific frame properties.
  • Limited flight dynamics simulations to be improved upon in the future, since I'm taking a flight dynamics course after this aircraft was designed.
  • 3D printing lightweight PLA can be challenging to tune and design for.
